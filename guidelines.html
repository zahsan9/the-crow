Research Abstract

A concise yet powerful summary of a longer, larger piece of work. Typically 300 words or fewer, an abstract distills the core elements of a paper so a reader can quickly assess its purpose, scope, and value without reading the full text.

Must cover

  • Thesis The central argument or claim of the full paper
  • Background Context that motivates the research
  • Results Key findings or outcomes
  • Conclusion What the results mean and what was learned

Researched Argumentative Essay

An original work that outlines an argument based on extensive review of previously published material. It goes beyond summarizing sources by taking an innovative stance and making a compelling case supported by research evidence.

Must cover

  • Thesis A clear, arguable central claim
  • Literature Thorough review and critical evaluation of related material
  • Argument An innovative, well-supported position on the topic
  • Citations In-text citations throughout and a full reference page

Common in the humanities, but may draw on quantitative or qualitative data from any discipline.

Research Editorial

A concise commentary on a currently published article or issue that highlights exciting recent research. Editorials do not primarily discuss the author's own work. Instead, they add context, explain interdisciplinary significance, or outline future research directions.

Must

  • Add dimension Go beyond summarizing the experiments or findings
  • Provide context Situate the work within the broader field
  • Look forward Identify future directions or interdisciplinary implications

Researched Literary Analysis

An essay evaluating an author's piece of work by closely examining their perspective, word choice, and writing structure. While typically focused on a single work, it may include additional primary texts and secondary scholarly sources to support the analysis.

Scope

  • Primary focus One work, though additional texts may be included
  • Analysis Perspective, language, structure, and literary devices
  • Sources Both primary and secondary sources are welcome

Research Paper

Presents findings from the student's own scholarly inquiry on a specific topic. Research papers are centered around a research question and include clearly defined methods, results, and conclusions drawn from the data collected.

Must cover

  • Question A focused, answerable research question
  • Methodology How data was collected and analyzed
  • Results What was found, with discussion of significance
  • Conclusions Interpretations drawn directly from the data

Accepts quantitative or qualitative data from any discipline.

Research Proposal

A document that puts forth a research question, a hypothesis, and a rationale for how that question will be investigated. Proposals include a literature review and a detailed methodology. They conclude by addressing the study's implications, significance, and limitations.

Must cover

  • Question Research question and hypothesis
  • Literature Review of existing research on the topic
  • Method Proposed approach, including ethical considerations if human subjects are involved
  • Significance Implications, importance, and limitations of the proposed study

Literature Review

Both a summary and a synthesis. A literature review does not contribute original findings; instead, it surveys existing scholarship on a topic, links ideas across sources, and identifies gaps or unresolved questions in the current body of research.

Key distinction

  • Summarize Accurately represent what existing sources say
  • Synthesize Draw connections and patterns across multiple sources
  • Identify gaps Highlight what is missing or unresolved in current research
  • Not original Does not introduce new data or original findings

Work in Progress

A report of findings from ongoing research that has not yet reached completion. A WIP contributes to the field even without final conclusions, sharing the hypothesis, method, and any preliminary results gathered so far.

Must cover

  • Hypothesis The central question or prediction guiding the research
  • Method The research approach being used
  • Preliminary results Included if available, but not required

Submitting a WIP does not prevent later publication of the completed paper in another venue.
