Letter From the Editors

The CROW showcases the initiative and commitment of students at the University of Washington Bothell to engage in their own research endeavors. As the reader, you will discover a myriad of topics ranging from the biological sciences to interdisciplinary arts and beyond. The multiple submission types and diverse writing styles are likely to capture the interest of any reader.

Conducting research is a dynamic, influential learning practice promoted throughout the University. Research solidifies learning outside of the classroom setting by allowing students to think critically about the topics they desire to explore, and to practice producing knowledge for themselves and others. Having their research published will forever preserve their work while also transforming it into a powerful tool to be used by the next generation of academics. By taking the monumental and often daunting step of participating in the peer-review process, the authors featured in this journal have emerged as contributors to the academic discourse of their particular field of study.

The Editorial Board was privileged to have the opportunity to review all of the incredible submissions this year. The selection process was undertaken thoughtfully with respect and sensitivity for all of the insightful, articulate submissions we received. We sincerely thank and commend all of the students for the dedication they put into their research and to everyone who submitted their work. We also thank the faculty and staff mentors who foster students' passions and talents. In this volume we proudly present the work of a growing research community.

2019 publications

  • The Effect of BPA on Gene Expression Biology

    ABSTRACT: The levels of the plastic chemical bisphenol A (BPA) that leach into food via plastic packaging have been deemed safe for public consumption by the FDA (2014). However, scientists have recently begun studying how BPA affects gene methylation, which controls when the DNA template is read so that gene function is only expressed when needed. BPA toxins absorbed in the body are found to cause gene demethylation where genes are then overexpressed — a problem when heavily regulated genes are affected, such as those coding for replication and growth. This review compares the percent of methylation change of four genes before and after treatment with 50 μg/kg of BPA, finding that three of four genes showed decreased methylation, supporting that BPA decreases gene methylation and could persuade the FDA to consider new regulations for BPA use.

    By Rachel Calder
  • Cosmic Strings: The Cracks in the Universe
    Astronomy Physics

    ABSTRACT: This research editorial covers the topic of cosmic strings, theoretical one-dimensional faults in space-time formed in the immediate aftermath of the Big Bang. Of particular note are oscillating cosmic string loops, which produce gravitational waves detectable by instruments such as LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational wave Observatory), and gravitational lensing, a visual distortion of distant objects potentially caused by the interference of cosmic strings in the foreground of images. While conclusive data on the phenomenon remains elusive, the potential insights into cosmology, particle physics, and other fields keep the search alive.

    By Hannah Preisinger
  • Education for Native Americans – Help or Hindrance?
    Education Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Native American students taught using a Euro-centric educational curriculum continue to fail out of the PreK-12 system. This paper explores how devaluing Indigenous culture, yet maintaining the expectation that Native American students will thrive and graduate, is harmful and ineffective. It asks how relationships between teachers and Native students — built with respect for and consideration of Indigenous culture — can lead to student success, arguing for curriculum reform that centers Indigenous knowledge and community.

    By Joyce Chester
  • Using Sci-Fi to Observe the Effects of Social Media and Reality TV Literature

    ABSTRACT: Science-fiction has a reputation for doing two things remarkably well: creating commentary on present-day societies and being eerily good at predicting possible technologies and futures. This paper utilizes pieces of science-fiction from various mediums to establish an observable pattern amongst writers in the genre today, and what these pieces say about the current state of reality television and social media. It also questions whether the ubiquitous presence of reality television and social media are perpetuating the normalization of using others as our own personal entertainment.

    By Brynley Louise
  • The Effects of Mass Incarceration on Individuals and Communities Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Over two million individuals are incarcerated in the United States. This paper examines the negative impacts of incarceration on individuals and their communities. Since most of the individuals affected by high rates of incarceration are people of color, this produces harmful effects in communities of color. The negative impacts include decreased social control within communities, higher rates of medical problems, decreased well-being, and relationship problems. Minimizing these impacts is a goal that doesn't address the systemic racism and poverty perpetuated by incarceration — a widespread change can only be achieved through a change in attitude toward race, crime, and punishment.

    By Ashley Forsyth
  • Giving Up the Ghost: The Parallel Demotion of Spirits and Spirituality Literature

    ABSTRACT: This paper examines the progression of Western attitudes towards ghosts and related concepts from ancient times through Europe's age of Enlightenment. Through a study of folklore, literature, and religious beliefs, a gradual shift becomes evident: from the sincere respect of ancestor veneration, to demonic paranoia in the Middle Ages, to public scorn and private curiosity as Western scientific traditions gained dominance. The author also proposes a parallel between this demotion of spirits and a diminishment in the societal importance of spirituality itself.

    By Hannah Preisinger
  • The Opioid Epidemic: Shifting from War to Peace
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: The misleading and unethical marketing practices of pharmaceutical companies during the late 90s led to a health crisis referred to as the opioid epidemic. As the rates of opioid usage skyrocketed along with rates of bloodborne illnesses due to needle sharing, the United States government's policy of extreme criminalization served to worsen the problem. Research into alternative policies yielded positive results — programs such as needle exchanges, rehabilitation, and decriminalization were each effective in remedying different consequences of the epidemic. However, because of the complexity of this issue, the United States must implement most or all of these policies together alongside a shift from a private to a public healthcare system.

    By AJ True
  • Infallibility Through Duality Literature

    ABSTRACT: This paper examines the puzzling interplay of Christianity and paganism in the English epic Bēowulf, adopting a psychoanalytical approach to explain the blending of two seemingly mutually exclusive faiths. Applying Saunder L. Gilman's theory of stereotyping, an extension of Lacanian psychology, this essay analyzes the Bēowulf-poet's motives for including such opposing religions, taking into consideration the time period's rocky transition from Saxon paganism to Christianity. Bēowulf may in fact be a unique window into the battle between the faiths — a singular moment in history captured in the person of its stalwart protagonist, shedding light on a virtually undocumented period of Western history.

    By Jakob Johnson
  • How Mutations in Synapsin I and Synapsin II Contribute to Seizure-Like Activity and Episodes in Individuals with Epilepsy
    Biology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: Epilepsy is one of the most prevalent neurological disorders and has been associated with abnormalities in the functioning of synapse proteins known as synapsins. This literature review synthesizes the work of three different articles to develop our understanding of how mutations in Synapsin I and Synapsin II may be correlated to seizure-like activity and episodes in individuals with epilepsy. Findings show that mutations in Synapsins I and II, individually or simultaneously, can contribute to the chronic seizure-like activity and episodes that individuals with epilepsy suffer — work of vital importance for informing new pharmacological methods for treating epileptic episodes in the future.

    By Atif Bhatti
  • The Effects of Youth Participatory Action Research (YPAR) on Leadership Development Among Youth
    Education Psychology

    ABSTRACT: Youth Participatory Action Research (YPAR) is a critical form of action research used in educational curriculum that focuses on and values the youth point of view. Based from action research alongside community member action, YPAR can be a powerful process that unites youth and adults in research projects that improve communities and the conditions of youth lives. The information that results from the YPAR process is meant to be used to advocate for change in communities, with this paper examining how YPAR participation shapes leadership development and civic engagement among young people.

    By Anna Tran
  • College Relationships and Grades
    Psychology Education

    ABSTRACT: Emerging adulthood is when many college students experience reduced parental involvement and increasing adult responsibilities including friendship, family, work, and academic obligations. This proposal explores the association of dating and grades in college among college-age students in Washington State's universities. Using a cross-sectional design with an online survey, the proposal asks demographic and dating-related questions alongside academic success questions such as grades received and hours studied each week. Results are expected to yield differences among year in college, how long students have been in a relationship, and academic success.

    By William Appel
  • Do Socioeconomic Factors Contribute to A Patient Having a Hospital Acquired Infection in Washington?
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Throughout history there has been a strong link between healthcare outcomes and socioeconomic status. Despite this, little to no data exists discussing how socioeconomic status affects hospital infection rates, even as hospital-acquired antibiotic resistant infections become a greater threat each year. To investigate this relationship, this proposal suggests a broad sampling of 10 Washington State hospitals, randomly selecting consenting patients and following up afterward to determine if they contracted an infection resulting from their hospital stay. It is imperative that we continue to investigate and better understand the role socioeconomic status plays on health outcomes.

    By Michael Prothman & Alex Wang
  • Depression and Exercise: Does Exercise Help People with Depression?
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: Depression is a worldwide health problem experienced by many individuals throughout all age groups, particularly youth. If not managed, depression can lead an individual to commit suicide. Studies have shown that exercise is an effective alternative in the management and treatment of depression. This proposal looks into how depression in college and university students can be successfully managed with exercise, using the Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ-9) to study depression levels alongside various types and intensities of exercise. The proposal will reveal that individuals who manage their depression with exercise see positive results and have more control over their symptoms.

    By Ameena Mohammed
  • The Effect of the Great Permian Extinction on Survival of Chemosymbiotic Bivalves: How Alternate Diets and Metabolic Processes Allow Organisms to Survive Marine Hypoxia Biology

    ABSTRACT: As fertilizer runoff and other nitrogen rich pollutants cause dead zones in the ocean, there are many implications for what types of organisms will survive modern marine hypoxia. This project determines whether the Permian-Triassic Extinction approximately 252 million years ago caused mass marine extinction via hypoxia, and whether chemosymbiotic bivalves — which survive by hosting chemosynthetic bacteria — were shielded from the event. Using data from the Paleobiology Database, the number and distribution of chemosymbiotic bivalves was compared across the late Permian and early Triassic. Contrary to the hypothesis, the Permian extinction did significantly affect the number of chemosymbiotic species — their numbers actually increased following the event.

    By Katherine Lu
  • Canid and Human Cohabitation: Canids did not Move into Caves with Humans Biology

    ABSTRACT: It is not known how long dogs and humans have had a relationship. If dogs and humans coexisted in the past, their fossils should be located in the same places they both resided — as canids developed a relationship with humans, it would make sense that they moved into caves when humans did. Data was obtained from the Paleobiology Database and sorted by time period, then further sorted depending on if the fossil was found in a cave. A χ²-test found that canids did not move into caves along with humans; in fact, there was a statistically significant difference supporting a possible negative relationship, suggesting a coexisting relationship may not have existed during those time periods given how recently canids were domesticated.

    By Lauren Kirk
  • Microbial Susceptibilities and Resistances to Campylobacter Jejuni Isolates from Crow Fecal Matter in Washington State
    Biology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: This research investigates the antimicrobial resistance profile of Campylobacter jejuni (C. jejuni) isolates obtained from crows within Bothell, Everett, Mercer Island, and Factoria, Washington. Antibiotic resistance is a growing public health issue — resistance to some antibiotics defeats the purpose of having antibiotics for treatment. Using the Kirby-Bauer method with eleven antibiotics, forty C. jejuni isolates from crow feces were tested. The isolates showed maximum resistance to ciprofloxacin (34.21%) and tetracycline (31.5%), with sixteen isolates showing multiple drug resistance. These results show that crows are capable of spreading antibiotic resistance through the bacteria C. jejuni, serving as environmental indicators of the antibiotic usage of the places they visit.

    By Neha Chhabra
