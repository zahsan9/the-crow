Letter From the Editors

The CROW highlights the incredible dedication of students at the University of Washington Bothell to conduct and compose their own research endeavors. As the reader, you will discover a myriad of topics ranging from the biological sciences to the interdisciplinary arts, as well as issues pertaining to our own campus. The multiple submission types and diverse writing styles will spark the interest of any reader.

The act of conducting research proves to be a highly impactful learning practice thoroughly promoted through the University. Research engages students outside of the classroom setting, allows them to think critically about the topics they desire to explore, and to practice producing knowledge for themselves and others. Having their work published will forever preserve their work, while also transforming it into a powerful tool to be used by the next generation of academics seeking new ideas.

By taking this monumental and often daunting step of subjecting their work to be reviewed and critiqued by their peers, the authors featured in this journal have progressed towards becoming contributors to the academic discourse of their particular field of study. The Editorial Board was overjoyed to have the opportunity to review all of the incredible submissions this year. We thank and praise all of the students for the hard work they put into their research, and we also thank the faculty and staff mentors who foster students' passions and talents. In this volume we proudly present the work of a growing research community.

2018 publications

  • UW Bothell Commuter Crisis Political Science

    ABSTRACT: This paper is a feasibility study analyzing the proposed potential solutions to the UW Bothell commuter crisis, including increased alternative transportation options, building new parking structures and lots, and a shuttle system to transport students to and from a lot off campus. By examining this important campus issue, this paper sheds light on the drawbacks to the proposed solutions assessed by administration — namely environmental implications, community impacts, and cost impacts. The results determined that the options currently on the table all have numerous benefits and drawbacks, and that building a parking structure could have many detrimental consequences, putting the campus further away from its climate neutrality goals.

    By Alexa Russo
  • Evidence that the Saber-tooth did not Increase the Lifespan of the Carnivora Species Biology

    ABSTRACT: There are many extant species from the Carnivora order, however, none of them are considered saber-toothed. To evaluate whether having saberteeth correlates to extinction, this study tests the hypothesis that non-saber-toothed species of Carnivora on average survived longer than saber-toothed species. Using data from the Paleobiology Database, the average lifespan of saber-toothed and non-saber-toothed species were compared. Results indicated a statistical significance between the average lifespans, supporting the research hypothesis — non-saber-toothed species existed longer than saber-toothed species of Carnivora, likely due to the lack of evidence indicating the usefulness of the saber-tooth over the smaller and stronger conical tooth.

    By Sydney Beaumont
  • Bats May have Originated in the Western Hemisphere Biology

    ABSTRACT: Although bats (order Chiroptera) make up one of the largest extant groups of mammals — second only to rodents — their location of origin remains a mystery. This paper describes an analysis of the bat fossil record made to narrow the location of origination for bats to either the Western or Eastern Hemisphere. The average age of fossils found within the Western Hemisphere was greater than those found in the Eastern Hemisphere (27.66 million years old vs. 17.99 million years old; p<0.001), indicating that bats most likely originated in the Western Hemisphere. However, the poor quality of the bat fossil record has limited the available data and could potentially affect the accuracy of the analysis.

    By Zachary Weldon
  • Cephalopod Evolution and the Increase in Size Across Mesozoic Periods Biology

    ABSTRACT: By looking at a species' change in morphology throughout time, it is possible to learn about the environmental conditions they lived in and what effect those conditions had on them. In this research paper, cephalopod widths have been examined to see if they underwent a noticeable change in size throughout the three Mesozoic periods — Jurassic, Triassic, and Cretaceous. The result is that cephalopods went through significant changes in size between every period in the Mesozoic. It is suggested that this increase in size was a response to environmental elements such as the Marine Mesozoic Revolution, the C/T extinction event, and other evolutionary factors.

    By Mudasair Zubair
  • Ammonoidea: An Exception to the Temperature Size Rule Biology

    ABSTRACT: A phenomenon exists where animals living in colder regions are larger than those living in warmer regions — scientists call this the temperature size rule. This study looks at whether ammonoids, an extinct subclass of cephalopods, are an exception to the rule due to their unique growth patterns and rapid evolution. A linear regression was run using width as the dependent variable and paleolatitude as the independent variable. The null hypothesis was not rejected due to a large p-value and small r² value, indicating a lack of relationship between the two variables. The conclusion is that ammonoids are an exception to the temperature size rule, and future studies should look at specific ammonoid species and explore additional variables such as depth.

    By Magdalena Brooks
  • Female Stereotyping in Vampire Fiction Literature

    ABSTRACT: This essay focuses on how negative stereotypes in the media can affect people's behaviors towards the groups targeted in films or books — specifically, how vampire fiction portrays females as psychotic and defined only by their bodies. The media can be highly influential and may shape our understanding of the world and what we believe everyone's role in it is. Analyzing vampire fiction and its portrayal of female characters is crucial in understanding why women may be treated with less dignity than men. This paper provides evidence that even subconsciously, the source of gendered assumptions we absorb from media shapes behavior, and that altering representation in books and films will be essential in moving society closer to gender equality.

    By Amman Nega
  • Health Care Access for Low-income Children
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Numerous studies have identified over 3.5 million uninsured children in the U.S. due to barriers of income, cost of insurance, and confusion about the insurance process. It is vital for children to obtain a regular source of health care during the crucial developmental stages of their young lives. This study investigates the obstacles low-income children face when trying to acquire health services in Snohomish County, Washington, and why children eligible for government assistance programs remain uninsured. The study aims to fill a gap in research about how poverty affects children's access to care and how assistance programs can be improved to better support low-income children across the U.S.

    By Thy Tran
  • Effects of 3,4-Methylenedioxymethamphetamine on Memory in Higher Education
    Health Studies Psychology Education

    ABSTRACT: MDMA is a psychoactive drug also known as ecstasy or "molly." As the number of raves and parties increases, the number of MDMA users continues to grow due to the drug's euphoria, hallucinogenic, and stimulant effects. This paper explores ways in which MDMA can affect the health of young adults at the University of Washington, and proposes a longitudinal experiment using a memory test to examine how MDMA affects the studies of students in higher education. The central question: do college students who take MDMA have worse memory problems than those who do not?

    By Thi Nguyen
  • Homeless Children
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: The purpose of this study was to examine the adverse psychological effects of homelessness on children and analyze how homelessness influences their behavior, school performance, and social-emotional and developmental functioning. Findings show that homelessness has a negative impact on children's psychological development. Frequent movement increases feelings of insecurity and creates severe psychological distress, potentially leading to anxiety, depression, social stress, and hostility — and researchers found that homelessness also interferes with the ability to learn. This study suggests it is essential to address the causes of poverty and homelessness in the lives of children and to develop interventions that stimulate empowerment for child personality and mother-child relationships.

    By Hanna Ashagre
  • Immigration and Healthcare Policies Creating Barriers for Undocumented Hispanic Immigrants
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Undocumented Hispanic immigrants face many healthcare disparities while living in the United States. A review of recent research shows negative impacts on mental and physical health statuses created by fear of deportation, lack of preventative healthcare options and insurance, and negative attitudes perpetuated by restrictive policies that target undocumented individuals. This proposal examines how restrictive immigration and healthcare policies have created challenges for undocumented immigrants and their families seeking access to healthcare, and demonstrates why additional research in this area is needed to identify barriers and reduce poor health outcomes.

    By Antoinette M. Smith
