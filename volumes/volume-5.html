Letter From the Editors

The CROW highlights the incredible dedication of students at the University of Washington Bothell to conduct, analyze, and synthesize their own research investigations. As the reader, you will discover a myriad of topics ranging from Science and Technology to the Interdisciplinary Arts and a few in between. The variety of submissions received showcase the diverse styles and research subjects that will attract readers from all backgrounds.

The act of conducting research is proven to be a highly impactful learning practice that engages students outside of the classroom setting and allows them to think more critically about the topics they wish to discover. Students present their ability to showcase and preserve their work, while also using it as a way to redesign and bring prospective to future academic research and endeavors. By taking the initiative to submit their work to be reviewed and critiqued by their peers, the authors in this journal have taken steps towards becoming active contributors to the academic discourse of their particular field of study.

The Editorial Board was delighted to have the opportunity to carefully review the overwhelmingly positive and well written submissions we received this year. We want to praise all the students for the hard work they put into their research and to everyone who submitted their work for review. We also want to thank the faculty and staff mentors who foster students' passions and talents, while working with them to develop into becoming published researchers.

2020 publications

  • Ayurveda: An Ancient Science for the Modern World
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: This editorial dives into the applications of Ayurveda to manage stress and anxiety, which include the benefits of oil massage, Shirodhara, yoga, and meditation. In the past few decades, pioneers of this system such as Harish Johari, Dr. Vasant Lad, David Frawley and also scientific research present that Ayurveda can be a complementary medicine to Western medicine. Ayurveda, known as the "Mother of All Healing" despite being an ancient healing art, has many uses today when searching for mental balance.

    By Karina Syrova
  • 30 Ppt Yields Highest Rate of Limb Regrowth in Luidia Clathrata Biology

    ABSTRACT: Sea stars have the ability to self-amputate limbs in the presence of danger (known as autotomy). How quickly those limbs can be regrown is vital to their survival, since having fewer limbs compromises sea stars' ability to hunt. This comparative study looked at two research papers testing how variations in the surrounding salinity affect the amount of limb regrown. The studies tested salinities of 20, 25, and 30 ppt; both found that 30 ppt yielded the most limb regrowth per unit time — recording 1.6 mm/week and 2.0 mm/week respectively. As average salinity fluctuations have increased by 5.3% globally since 1950, the effects of salinity on limb regrowth is becoming an increasingly urgent subject of research.

    By Jakob Johnson
  • Men and Masculinities: A Case Study of Mass Shootings in the United States ⚠ Content Warning
    Political Science Psychology

    ABSTRACT: Research suggests there may be a link between mass shootings and the values imposed by hegemonic masculinity. The vast majority of the men who commit mass murder adhere to strict gender norms that prove to be unsustainable, resorting to violence as a way to reassert their masculinity and prove their status. While hegemonic masculinity does not directly cause mass murder, studies have proposed it is present within the factors that exacerbate it — primarily rape culture's willingness to dismiss violence against women. This paper explores the cases of Seung Hui-Cho, Robert Dear and Elliot Rodger, focusing on how hegemonic masculinity can lead men to commit gratuitous acts of violence and what this means for prevention.

    By Elisabeth Schnebele
  • Sustainable Viniculture: Giving Salmon a Chance to Thrive for Future Generations
    Biology Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Conventionally farmed vineyards use excessive amounts of water and chemicals, and their negative environmental impacts — including chemical runoff and soil erosion — kill vulnerable salmon eggs incubating in satellite streams and rivers. Salmon populations in the Salish Sea have declined dramatically since first recorded in 1984. Multiple international universities have identified viable agricultural techniques for creating sustainable vineyards that preserve water, lessen the need for chemical pesticides, and prevent soil erosion. An additional benefit is vintners do not have to spend money on these items, thereby increasing profit margins. Recent surveys also reveal consumers are willing to pay more for sustainably produced wines, making the ethical and economic case for adoption.

    By April Oertle
  • Fentanyl for Respiratory Distress Management in Hospice Settings Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: This nonsystematic literature review examines whether fentanyl has greater efficacy compared to morphine for management of respiratory distress in adult patients with terminal illness in hospice settings. Fentanyl delivers faster onset for relief of symptoms and offers diverse administration routes. Additionally, fewer side effects were noted that are commonly associated with opioid medications, including neurocognitive changes, drowsiness, dizziness, and nausea. With greater than fifty percent of patients experiencing dyspnea at the end of their life, addressing respiratory distress is critical to quality of care. Although there is evidence to support fentanyl's efficacy in managing dyspnea, more studies are necessary to quantify its significance and potential compared to other opioid medications.

    By Jessica Heitzman
  • 'LIKE' and the Cross-generational Colloquial Conundrum: The Rise of Hieroglyphs in Colloquial Language Literature

    ABSTRACT: Language barriers are found between generational groups more often than not, even when each group speaks the same language. Each generation adapts their language to suit their needs, influenced by the technological landscape and social norms. In the last thirty years colloquial phrases used by Millennials, such as "like," have brought about a resurgence of hieroglyphs — the antiquated concept of a picture standing in for a mood, tone, or series of phrases. By reshaping language to suit new communication needs, Millennials and Generation Z have created a new type of language barrier between themselves and Generation X. Tracing new uses of the word "like" in social situations, writing, and spoken communication helps gauge community ties and communication strength within youth culture.

    By Grace Boulanger
  • "Pull Yourself Up by Your Bootstraps" an American Mythology
    Political Science Education

    ABSTRACT: In the United States, individual success does not equate a heightened level of effort, nor is success more likely if one works harder. Phrases such as "pull yourself up by your bootstraps" are antiquated manifestations of systemic oppression primarily directed at communities of color. This ideology is damaging because it masks itself as harmless encouragement while perpetuating systems of victim blaming, racial and gendered stereotypes, and invalidating the lived experience of those affected by oppression. The ideology of "working harder" is an invalid solution for overcoming systematic adversity due to poverty's cyclical nature, discrepancies in quality of and access to education, and the existence of racial and gender wage gaps.

    By Gabrielle Fox
  • Policy Brief: Mandatory Vaccinations for Public Health
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Once considered a triumph of public health, immunizations have fallen victim to rumor and skepticism while scientific evidence takes a back seat. Washington state legislation needs to take action and eliminate all non-medical exemptions for vaccinations. Washington has recently reacted to a nationwide measles outbreak by tightening exemptions on the MMR vaccine, but will this always be a reactionary response? The dangers of not instituting stricter requirements include outbreaks of preventable contagious diseases, susceptibility of the medically vulnerable, and extraordinary healthcare costs — estimated at $9 billion annually for vaccine-preventable diseases in the U.S. Requiring immunizations of the young will target whole generations and substantiate a foundation for disease eradication.

    By Jessica Heitzman
  • Racial Disparities in Acute Myeloid Leukemia Survival Rates: A Mixed Method Analysis of Contributing Factors
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: In the United States, leukemia cancer patients of color continue to have lower overall survival outcomes compared to Caucasian leukemia patients, yet existing research does not detail what factors are contributing to this disparity. This proposal aims to explore the impact of variables such as transportation access, insurance status, and stage of cancer at diagnosis on various populations' ability to survive the cancer. Using a mixed method longitudinal study design with online survey elements, patients receiving active care for leukemia will be selected randomly from four cancer treatment centers in the Greater Seattle area and followed for five years. The results are expected to yield greater understanding of factors that decrease leukemia survival rates in populations of color, with the intent of influencing better care and outcomes for those populations.

    By Aaron Davis
  • The Effect of Neurotoxins in Vaccines and Pregnancy Factors on Autism Spectrum Disorder
    Health Studies Biology

    ABSTRACT: This study proposal evaluates the relative strength of associations of neurotoxin exposure from vaccinations and parental health factors with the risk of autism spectrum disorder in offspring. The prevalence of autism spectrum disorder has recently increased in the U.S. Infants aged up to 6 months old have 14.7 to 49 times greater neurotoxin exposure than U.S. safety limits from parental aluminum-adjuvanted vaccines. A 74% increased risk of ASD in offspring was found from pre-gestational diabetes, and children of obese mothers showed a 36% higher risk relative to children of mothers at normal weight. A case-control study design will be implemented evaluating non-probability convenience sampling from three hospitals in Washington State, studying 120 children with ASD and 180 without.

    By Tyler Youn
  • Effectiveness of Clinical Drug Treatments for Alzheimer's Disease in Slowing Progression of Memory Loss & Cognitive Function Loss
    Health Studies Biology

    ABSTRACT: Currently, Aricept, Ebixa, Exelon, and Lamivudine are the drugs used to slow the progression of Alzheimer's Disease. These drugs are used to slow the symptoms of memory loss and cognitive function loss. This proposal investigates the effectiveness of these drugs through administering memory tests, cognitive tests, surveys, PET scans, and CT scans to Alzheimer's Disease patients. The drugs tested will be considered effective if there is evidence of a slowdown in memory loss and cognitive function loss, with findings intended to inform future treatment decisions and pharmaceutical research.

    By Allen Lewis
  • DSHS Care Tool Mental Health Assessment Analysis: Is it Adequate In Washington State?
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: The Comprehensive Assessment Reporting and Evaluation (CARE) Tool is a large scale holistic assessment currently used by Washington State's Department of Social and Health Services (DSHS) to determine care eligibility for people who may need long-term community services. The CARE Tool has been found to not be sensitive to the psychological signs and symptoms present when someone is first diagnosed with traumatic brain injury. It only assesses patients based on the day of assessment and seven days prior, and is not designed to predict potential significant changes a patient may need in the next 1 to 2 years. A literature review found multiple other behavioral and mental health assessment tools that are more sensitive to milder psychological signs and symptoms, concluding that the CARE Tool's behavioral and MMSE assessments need to be changed through policy action.

    By Kimberly Rice
  • Obstetric Outcomes Among Women of The Navajo Nation: A Mixed Method Study Exploring Cultural Humility and Maternal Health Within Indian Health Services
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: American Indian/Alaskan Native (AI/AN) women are the first people of North America and yet often the first to be overlooked when it comes to maternal health, due to a complicated history of broken treaties and the long-lasting impacts of colonization. Their life expectancy is over five times less than that of the U.S. population. This study uses an exploratory sequential design to build a baseline for maternal health in the Navajo Nation, pulling and de-identifying 2019 birth data from four IHS hospitals and interviewing ten Navajo women about their childbirth experiences. The research strives to support and empower Navajo women by ensuring their voice is heard in discussions about maternal health outcomes and care.

    By Angelina Phoebe Keryte & Dacia Wagnon
  • Airtime
    Literature Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: This paper shows that certain continuous assaults on individuality by the technologies of the medical-industrial complex's surveillance state can be overcome by rejecting conformity and reclaiming the self through a methodology orthogonal to the attack. Beginning with practical and physical aspects of CPAP usage — including treatment alternatives, living with the device, and social impacts — the paper explores aspects of gamification, control, and privacy involving the transition of intimate data to a corporate or public environment. It then expands the discussion of airflow aesthetically and metaphorically to include alternative airflows such as dreaming and singing, concluding with a creative intervention to retain personal autonomy and identity.

    By Cliff Watson
  • Myths and Gender in Avatar: The Last Airbender Literature

    ABSTRACT: This exploration of Avatar: The Last Airbender (2005–2008) analyzes several characters to discover how they accept and reject the norms associated with their gender. By using signs to explain how viewers determine each character's gender, attention is called to the norms associated with certain gender identities. Among other social issues, the lens of fourth wave feminism values the pluralism of gender identity which expands beyond a traditional womxn-man binary. In looking at how each character interacts with the world around them despite their gender, we can determine how those actions align with — and stray from — the norms associated with their gender.

    By Katie Sue Eichner
  • Pokémon Go: The Unknown Truth About Players with a Mental or Physical Disability
    Psychology Education

    ABSTRACT: Pokémon GO is an augmented reality mobile game where players must physically move around in populated areas to catch Pokémon and find Pokéstops for digital rewards. This study looks at whether there is a difference in hours played each week between players who have a physical or mental disability versus those who do not. A t-test was run using hours played as an independent variable and disability status as the dependent variable. Rejection of the null hypothesis supported the prediction that Pokémon GO players who have a disability play fewer hours each week. Future studies should explore comparing other restrictions involving people with disabilities to better understand what accommodations would help create augmented reality games where everyone can be successful.

    By Emily Person
  • Augmented Reality Games and Accessibility
    Psychology Education

    ABSTRACT: With the rapid growth of the gaming industry, it is vital to focus on whether games being produced are accessible to players with disabilities. Players with physical disabilities struggle to use game dynamics designed for individuals with full visual-motor coordination, and players with mental disabilities have not been adequately considered in accessibility research. This study looks at whether the social interactivity dynamics of the AR game Ingress are accessible to players with physical and mental disabilities. A chi-squared test comparing observed and expected player responses to 7 questions evaluating social interactivity yielded a p-value of 0.3481, leading to the conclusion that Ingress has adequate accommodations for players with mental and physical disabilities — providing a potential model for future accessible AR game development.

    By Lindsey McCormick
  • Searching for Trends in the Atmospheres of Exoplanets
    Astronomy CSSE

    ABSTRACT: The field of exoplanets has been experiencing rapid growth alongside the technological advances made since exoplanets were first discovered in the 1990s — with more than 4,100 confirmed to date. Several of these exoplanets have had their upper atmospheres probed with transmission spectroscopy. The goal of this research is to gather previously published transmission spectroscopy data in order to characterize trends relating exoplanets' physical properties to their atmospheric compositions. The study focuses on hot Jupiters with periods of less than 3.5 Earth days and radii between one and three times the radius of Jupiter, presenting results on the 24 exoplanets with spectral data in the wavelength range of 3000–9000 Å.

    By Wynter Broussard, Hielen Enyew, & Shushmitha Radjaram
