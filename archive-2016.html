Letter From the Editors

The CROW highlights the incredible dedication of students at the University of Washington Bothell to conduct, analyze, and synthesize their own research investigations. As the reader, you will discover a myriad of topics ranging from Science and Technology to the Interdisciplinary Arts and a few in between. The multiple submission types and various writing styles feature the campus research and observational writings that should spark any reader's interests.

The act of conducting research is proven to be a highly impactful learning practice that engages students outside of the classroom setting and allows them to think more critically about the topics they wish to discover. Having their work published will forever encapsulate and preserve their work while also transforming it into a powerful tool which can be used by the next academic who seeks new ideas.

By taking this monumental and often daunting step of subjecting their work to be reviewed and critiqued by their peers, the authors featured in this journal have progressed towards becoming contributors to the academic discourse of their particular field of study. The Editorial Board was overjoyed to have had the opportunity to review all the incredible submissions this year. We want to praise all the students for the hard work they put into their research and to everyone who submitted their work for review. We also want to thank the faculty and staff mentors who foster students' passions and talents.

  • Understanding the Origins of Animal Multicellularity Through the Studies of Choanoflagellates Biology

    ABSTRACT: Choanoflagellates are the closest living relative of animals, confirmed through multiple lines of phylogenetic analyses, comparative genomics, and similarities in cell biology. Previous studies of the choanoflagellate Salpingoeca rosetta suggest that bacteria may have played an important role in the early origins of animals. One study showed that the Bacteriodetes Algoriphagus machinoponensis can release lipid signaling molecules that induce choanoflagellates to grow into multicellular colonies known as rosettes. Another showed that when grown in the presence of Vibrio fischerii, choanoflagellates will exhibit swarming behavior and sexually reproduce. Through the use of molecular and comparative genomic approaches, choanoflagellates offer an experimentally tractable, phylogenetically relevant model system for investigating the unicellular ancestry of animals.

    By Isaac Kim
  • Food Environment in Healthcare
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: With chronic disease prevalence rates continually increasing, a public health response in prevention is needed. Dietary and nutritional behavior that prevents disease is directly linked to the quality of food consumed, yet hospital administrations are signing contracts with fast food corporations and processed food suppliers, skewing an assumed alliance with healthy living requirements for chronic disease patients. Medical students receive very little nutrition education in medical training, and medical administrations are failing to educate and motivate patients. Moving forward, changes in policy development, healthcare food environments, and medical school nutrition education can realign with public health disease prevention and health promotion initiatives — allowing medical facilities to act as a point of public health intervention and build trust with patients and the community.

    By Samantha Frati, Jessica Jacobsen, Rachel Li, & Shelby Lubchuk
  • Livelihood Identity: How Food is Used as Resistance in the Mangrove Ecosystems of Ecuador
    Political Science Biology

    ABSTRACT: Since the late 1960s, the development of the shrimp-farm industry in Ecuador has contributed to extensive ecological damage to mangrove areas, severely threatening the livelihood of those reliant on these ecosystems. In response, the population mobilized a national grassroots movement that used food to articulate resistance against the shrimp-farm industry and the Ecuadorian government. The "livelihood identity," or collective sense of belonging, among ancestral mangrove people has motivated communities to protect not only their food source, but also their way of life. This paper analyzes how the expansion of the shrimp-farm industry has altered resource availability and access in mangrove communities and given rise to social movements that utilize food culture as a tool of resistance.

    By Hillary Sanders
  • Analysis of CO2, CO, PM2.5, and PM10 From Flaming and Smoldering Combustion in a Home Wood Stove
    Physics Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: Carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, and particulate matter smaller than 2.5μm have known negative health and climate implications, making them advantageous to study. Three instruments — a trace level CO analyzer, a non-dispersive infrared gas analyzer for CO2, and an aerosol monitor for particulate matter — were used to continuously measure the output of these pollutants from a wood stove under two types of combustion: flaming and smoldering. The CO instrument has a lower detectable limit of 0.04ppm; the CO2 instrument has a measurement range of 0–20,000ppm with accuracy <3%; and the DustTrak uses gravimetric and photometric analysis to measure concentrations from 0.001–150 mg/m³, providing comprehensive pollutant profiles for both combustion modes.

    By Sara Wells
  • Overexpression, Purification, and Inhibition of Helicobacter Pylori Aldo-keto Reductase (HpAKR) using Designer Inhibitors
    Biology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: Helicobacter pylori infects the gastric mucosa of over half of the world's population and is implicated in the genesis of many gastric pathologies. Current treatments are becoming increasingly ineffective as antibiotic-resistant strains become more prevalent. The purpose of this project is to discover a competitive inhibitor for an aldo-keto reductase enzyme (HpAKR) that is required for H. pylori to survive in the human stomach. The plasmid containing the HpAKR gene will be transformed into Rosetta™ E. coli cells for overexpression, and the purified HpAKR molecules will be assayed for activity and inhibition, with findings intended to inform future antibiotic-resistant treatment strategies.

    By Taryn Meachem
  • Analysis of Fluoride, Chloride, Carbonate, and Sulfate in Filtered, Tap, and Ground Water Samples by ISE and Titration
    Biology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: This study compares natural groundwater to tap water and filtered water to identify whether groundwater has significantly different concentrations of fluoride, chloride, carbonate, and sulfate (FCCS). Consuming high concentrations of fluoride can lead to fluorosis and reduction of IQ, neither of which have effective treatment options. Samples were collected from two Pacific Northwest households and compared to potable groundwater from the Artesian Well in Lynnwood, with fluoride and chloride measured via ion-selective combination electrode and sulfate and carbonate via titration. The largest source of error comes from hydroxide interference when detecting fluoride and chloride concentrations, informing recommendations for prevention of fluoride-related health issues.

    By Bunraj Grewal
  • Access & Affordability in Public Health Policy to Increase Adherence of Cancer Prevention Guidelines
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Children residing in low income, food insecure neighborhoods face increased obstacles in adhering to cancer prevention guidelines (CPGs), which focus on nutrition and physical activity to maintain a healthy body weight. Geographic, economic, structural, and social conditions place low socioeconomic status (SES) youth at increased risk. Results indicate that cancer prevalence is increasing globally, many cancers are preventable, and higher SES households adhere to CPGs more easily than those in low SES households. Children rarely meet daily fruit and vegetable recommendations, and both cancer survivors and families without a cancer history are largely unaware of CPGs. Targeted public health policies and programs with qualitative, community-specific approaches are needed to increase adherence and access within low SES communities.

    By Jessica Jacobson
  • Determining Potential Inhibitor(s) of Thioredoxin Glutathione Reductase, Key Enzyme of Schistosoma Mansoni Parasite
    Biology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: Schistosomiasis is a parasitic worm disease affecting more than 200 million people worldwide, and praziquantel remains the only treatment. With patients showing signs of resistance to praziquantel, there is an urgent need to develop a new alternative drug. This experiment will determine potential inhibitor(s) of Thioredoxin glutathione reductase (TGR), an essential enzyme responsible for the parasite's survival, using three phases of computational modeling: virtual screening, lead optimization, and down-selection. Top hit compounds are predicted to have binding affinities of about -10.0 kcal/mol after virtual screening, improving to -13.7 kcal/mol after lead optimization — with the strongest candidates progressing toward preclinical and clinical drug development.

    By Mengkhy Lay & Dr. Peter Anderson
  • Examining the Effects of Different Diets and Salinities on Copepod Population Growth Biology

    ABSTRACT: Climate impacts on coastal oceans — sea level rise, increased storm intensity, and increased precipitation — can lead to a rise in fresh water entering coastal ecosystems, causing decreases in ocean salinity. Sea urchins are key members of nearshore food webs and their feces may provide large amounts of available nutrients for other organisms. This research studied the population growth of T. californicus copepods in both low salinity and normal seawater environments, with diets of either fresh Ulva or urchin fecal Ulva. Both diet and salinity significantly affected population growth — low salinity proved to be the better environment, and fresh Ulva the better diet — with implications for understanding how marine food web dynamics will respond to climate-driven environmental changes.

    By Martha Raymore & Megan Dethier
  • Development of a Thermal Desorption and CRY-GC-MS Method for the Measurement of VOCs in Ambient Air Physics

    ABSTRACT: Wildfires have increased across North America in recent years. Acetonitrile (CH3CN) is chosen as a trace molecule because it indicates biomass burning while anthropogenic sources are limited. Previous studies required transport of massive equipment to fire sites, giving importance to developing a simple, portable, and inexpensive method for tracing acetonitrile production. This study proposes a new method using thermal desorption-cryofocusing-gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (TD-Cryo-GC-MS) with higher sensitivity, reproducibility, and recovery for measuring VOCs in ambient air. TD tubes packed with PorapakN adsorbent were used, with Na2SO4 showing 99.8% recovery as an ozone trap and breakthrough tests yielding 1.87 ±0.56% recovery for concentrations up to 60ng CH3CN over two hours. The resulting validation experiment demonstrates good first results and establishes a foundation for future measurement of wildfire-associated VOCs.

    By Angela Angelevska & Crystal McClure
