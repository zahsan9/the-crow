Letter From the Editors

The Eighth Edition of The CROW was composed and published in a time when the COVID-19 global pandemic began to shift to the past, and we students continued re-establishing our lives as in-person learners and learning communities. During this time, members of the University of Washington Bothell community continued to feel the impact of this transition in every aspect of our lives while still pursuing our education. Simultaneously, this time has been shaped by global events, fueling a generation of young adults dedicated to addressing our planet's persistent issues. This board feels it necessary to acknowledge the hardship and loss these past few years have brought to students, faculty, support staff, and everyone we are connected to, near and far. We acknowledge the effort it takes to be involved in changing our world and lives, so thank you foremost for being here a part of this work.

With this edition, The CROW continues to highlight the incredible dedication of students at UW Bothell as they conduct, analyze, and synthesize their own research investigations in topics ranging from science and technology, interdisciplinary arts, health studies, and everything in between. Conducting research is a high-impact learning practice that engages students outside the classroom and allows them to think more critically about the topics they wish to discover. By taking the initiative to submit their work for review by a board of their peers, the authors in this journal have taken steps towards becoming active contributors to academic discourse that values inclusion, expansion, and critical thinking.

The University of Washington Bothell is situated on the ancestral lands of the Coast Salish peoples, including the Duwamish and Snohomish tribes. We acknowledge this land's deep historical, cultural, and spiritual significance to Indigenous communities. We recognize the ongoing harm caused by colonization and affirm our responsibility as a student-led research publication to engage in decolonization efforts. The CROW seeks to create an inclusive and equitable publication that values Indigenous experience, knowledge, and culture, and fosters justice.

We would like to extend special recognition and gratitude to our student authors. In spite of fears of rejection, or perceived possible judgment by your peers, our authors showed great appreciation and care for the extensive editing and publication process, all during a difficult time for students — making their publication all the more extraordinary. We know submitting was difficult, and we appreciate your patience and effort. We are so excited to share the 2023 Edition of The CROW!

2023 Publications

  • The Effect of Advanced Age and Alzheimer's Disease Neuropathology on Levels of the Tight Junction Protein, Occludin, in the Brain Microvasculature
    Biology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: The Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) is a physiologic interface between the bloodstream and the brain. The BBB has tight junction proteins (TJPs) that restrict blood and toxins from entering the brain. This interface plays an important role in the health of our brains and helps protect against neurodegenerative diseases. A significant increase in the TJP occludin was found in microvessels isolated from the parietal lobe of female subjects with Alzheimer's Dementia relative to age-matched females without AD. The increase of occludin in female AD microvessels is an unexpected finding, and could reflect a compensatory mechanism within brain microvasculature in the context of both advanced age and Alzheimer's Dementia — potentially informing therapies to protect the BBB.

    By Ali Mirzazadeh
  • Unearthing the Environmental Consequences in Seattle's New Holly Neighborhood
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Seattle's New Holly neighborhood is home to a diverse community facing numerous environmental challenges. Due to historical redlining and systemic inequality, this community has been disinvested in and exposed to hazardous pollutants, putting them at risk of health issues and displacement. New Holly has some of the least clean air quality in one of the U.S.'s most polluted cities, and its residents are disproportionately affected by environmental contamination. New developments in the area encourage gentrification and displacement of low-income families. While community members and organizations try to address these issues, systemic and historical factors make public efforts necessary to advocate for change and create an equitable and healthy community.

    By Ishika Nayyar
  • From Virtual to Reality: An Economic Analysis of the Cloud Kitchen Industry Business

    ABSTRACT: Cloud kitchens have grown to a market size of $63.9B USD in 2022. The COVID-19 pandemic led to a surge in the market for these facilities as they proved to be a cost-effective option for restaurants to continue operating and meet the rising demand for food delivery. Cloud kitchens offer a range of economic benefits, including reduced costs, increased profitability, and decreased barriers to entry for new restaurant businesses. They also provide greater flexibility in food production and menu selections to meet the fluctuating customer demand. With the shift towards takeout and delivery likely to remain prevalent in the food service industry, cloud kitchens are expected to continue being an innovative and efficient way to meet the growing demand for online food ordering and delivery.

    By Mariam Badr
  • When Group Boundaries Blur: The Impact of Southeast Asian Representation in Film and Television
    Literature Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Representation of minorities in the media is a frequently touched upon topic, especially regarding racial minorities. While the media has made strides in increased representation of minorities over the years, the overall quantity and quality of representation in television and film is still lacking. This is especially true when it comes to the representation of Southeast Asians in media, as both media platforms and the general populace are too quick to group Southeast Asians, South Asians, and East Asians into a singular racial category. This research paper analyzes sources implicating the impact that the lack of quality representation in media has on Southeast Asians, to build an argument about media being a powerful tool that should be used in ways that will lead us to a more accepting and diverse world.

    By Anika Gopez
  • Domestic Violence: Laws and Regulations Are Discouraging Women from Reporting ⚠ Content Warning
    Political Science Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: The statistics show that in the United States, rates of domestic violence against women have not decreased over the years, and globally, the statistics look very similar. Every year, more research emerges causing the current laws around domestic violence to become outdated. Without evaluating these, the likelihood of women continuing to not report domestic violence instances will continue to grow. We must look at the laws around domestic violence and see where change can be made so that women, and men, and all people, feel comfortable to come forward in domestic violence situations.

    By Amy Walesby
  • The Social Determinants and Cultural Barriers in Obtaining a Mammography Screening for Southeast Asian Immigrant Women in Los Angeles: An Exploratory Sequential Study
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Mammogram screenings are X-rays of the breasts used to detect early indicators of breast abnormalities such as cancerous bumps. The leading cause of cancer death in Asian American women is breast cancer, yet Asian Americans have the lowest rates of mammogram screenings compared to all other racial groups. In order to tackle social determinants and barriers, Asian Americans cannot be seen as a homogenous group — research studies often exclude Southeast Asian ethnicities, glossing over determinants unique to each ethnicity such as income, immigrant status, and education level. This exploratory sequential study collects both qualitative and quantitative data to identify barriers Southeast Asian immigrant women in Los Angeles face, setting a baseline for public health professionals.

    By Saajidah Abideen
  • Aspects of Mental Health and the African American Community
    Health Studies Psychology Political Science

    ABSTRACT: From institutionalized discrimination to stigma, the Black community have faced many challenges to receive much-needed support and treatment for their mental illnesses. The main question revolves around whether increased awareness and accessibility to mental health resources impacts the quality of life for African Americans. The proposed study addresses three main obstacles that hinder African Americans from seeking help: stigma around mental illness, historical atrocities done to African Americans by the healthcare field, and the effects these injustices have on African Americans' decision to receive care. The anticipated outcome is that increased awareness will result in greater willingness to accept treatment, leading to an overall better quality of life.

    By Yordanos Berhane
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma: The Impact of Patient Experience Within the Diagnosis Process, a Qualitative Study
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: Cancer is known to be the second leading cause of death in the United States. Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC) is a brutal cancer that results in a minuscule number of survivors, yet no effective screening methods have been implemented to diagnose RCC prior to a metastatic stage. This research proposal will explore the patient experience of RCC patients — humanizing cancer patients to better understand how traditional medicine may have failed them prior to diagnosis. RCC patients cannot advocate for themselves in a system not currently made to support the detection of their disease. The end goal is to create a tool better at detecting RCC while the cancer is in its early stages.

    By Isabella M. Humphries
  • An Intervention on Free and Accessible Prenatal Care for Black Mothers in Low-Income Neighborhoods
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Inequities in maternal healthcare, especially for Black and Hispanic women, are a serious issue that is killing many lives. The United States has a relatively high prevalence of negative maternal mortality — 700 women pass away during pregnancy or within a year of giving birth, and two out of every three of these deaths might have been avoided. Socioeconomic status, lack of access to prenatal care, poor medical care, and persistent stress all contribute to this population's increased vulnerability. This study aims to ascertain how having optimal care prior to, during, and after pregnancy may affect the maternal mortality rate, and considers potential laws that would ensure equitable treatment for all.

    By Sinit Ogbagaber
  • Eating Disorder Symptoms Associated with Cultural Shifts in East Asian Cultures versus Western Culture
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: This essay discusses the presentation of Anorexia Nervosa (AN) symptoms in Chinese and Japanese cultures versus North American culture. It highlights the importance of recognizing eating disorders in more diverse populations and understanding how sociocultural factors influence their development. The symptoms of AN are explored through a cultural lens while comparing the varying symptoms against the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders. Noticing the symptomatic differences of AN in each culture shows that the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria of Anorexia Nervosa is not universal in all countries or cultures — indicating an increased need for mental health and medical professionals to acknowledge cultural influences when diagnosing and treating eating disorders.

    By Mary Kapitonenko
  • Panthera Latitudinal Ranges Increase Over Time Biology

    ABSTRACT: The explanations for the latitudinal diversity gradient (LDG), the increase of species from the equator to the poles, have long been debated by scientists for over a century. To inspect this concept further, the correlation between the big cat genus Panthera's fossil age and latitudinal range was examined to test the "out of the tropics" theory — that species disperse from the tropics over time. A linear regression test found that as Panthera fossils' age increases, so do their latitudinal ranges. These results further support the "out of the tropics" theory, meaning the LDG does indeed exist.

    By Molly Elise Biedscheid
  • The Progression of HIV and AIDS
    Health Studies Biology

    ABSTRACT: Since HIV was contracted by a human from a chimpanzee in the 1800s, more than 84.2 million people have been infected with HIV and 40.1 million have died from AIDS. Though this virus is powerful and has shown its ability to take over a portion of the population around the world, different steps like PrEP and PEP have been shown to decrease the rates as a preventive measure. PrEP and PEP are both medications that can be taken for either pre-exposure or post-exposure to HIV. With the evolution of medicine and the virus continuing to shift with science over time, the goal is to one day create a cure to prevent HIV and AIDS.

    By Brittany Howell
  • A Climate Change Adaptation Assessment of the State of Washington: Industrial Hemp & Building the Evergreen State of our Future
    Biology Political Science

    ABSTRACT: This Adaptation Assessment of the State of Washington highlights the great potential of industrial hemp as an agronomic defense powerhouse against climate change, helping humanity transition away from fossil fuel. In theory, Washington has the potential to sequester over 28 million tonnes of CO₂e annually if industrial hemp were incorporated into its existing crop cycles statewide. Industrial hemp has a myriad of known uses, including hemp-based plastics and fuel. Given the public perception of hemp and its controversial history in the United States, a lack of research remains — and its potential has yet to be fully recognized. Washington can change that, but it must start from the top down with good policy and urgent implementation.

    By Loren Herrera
  • Systematic Analysis of the Quasar Properties and Variability of Extremely-High Velocity Outflow Quasars in the Sloan Digital Sky Survey
    Physics Astronomy

    ABSTRACT: Active Galactic Nuclei host a supermassive black hole surrounded by in-falling gas in the form of an accretion disk. The most luminous of them, called quasars, regularly present outflowing winds observed as absorption in their spectra. We present the preliminary results on the first systematic study on Extremely High Velocity Outflow (EHVO) quasar properties and variability in the 16th data release of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey. Of the 98 quasars with EHVOs discovered, 51 cases were inspected for variability — 47% showed variability. We find that quasars with EHVOs exhibit larger values of bolometric luminosity and Eddington ratio compared to Broad Absorption Line Quasi-Stellar Objects and non-BAL parent sample quasars, while black hole mass does not show significant differences.

    By Easton Pierce, Alex Vong, Abby Wang, Cora DeFrancisco, Anish Rijal, Tzitzi Romo Pérez, Mikel Charles, Wendy García Naranjo, Rachel Fulda, Veronica Powell, & Lorena Sanabria
