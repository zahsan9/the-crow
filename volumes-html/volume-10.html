Letter From the Editors

This momentous Tenth Edition of The CROW was composed and published in the midst of major education funding cuts, and anxieties around changes in the country. As we witness ongoing attacks on the rights of our community members, we feel that the mission of The CROW to platform and uplift student voices is our way of advocating. We thank you for being part of this work and reaffirm our belief in the value and impact of student writing, student activism, and student investment in global futures.

With this edition, The CROW continues to highlight the incredible dedication of students at UW Bothell as they synthesize their own investigations in topics ranging from science and technology, interdisciplinary arts, health studies, and everything in between. Research is a high-impact learning practice that engages students outside the classroom and allows them to think more critically about the topics they wish to discover. By taking the initiative to submit their work for review by a board of their peers, the authors in this journal have taken steps towards becoming active contributors to academic discourse that values inclusion, expansion, and critical thinking. As ways of writing and research advance, student publications like The CROW lie on the frontier. This year saw us throwing our belated 23-24 launch party, working through several power outages, and fielding papers from a wide variety of disciplines.

We would like to extend special recognition and gratitude to our student authors. Despite fears of rejection, judgement, or critique; our authors showed perseverance with the editing and publication process. We know submitting was difficult, and we appreciate your patience and effort as we tried to ensure your work would shine. We are so excited to share the 2025 Edition of The CROW!

  • Evolution of American Foreign Policy: The Creation of a Global Superpower Political Science

    ABSTRACT: This paper examines the evolution of American foreign policy and its transformation from isolationism to global superpower status. By analyzing key historical developments, including 19th-century expansionism, involvement in the World Wars, and post-war international leadership, the paper explores how the United States strategically balanced isolationist tendencies with interventionist actions to secure its national interests. This study argues that territorial expansion, economic influence, and military engagements shaped America's rise, culminating in its dominant role in world affairs. Through foreign aid, military alliances, and global governance initiatives, contemporary U.S. foreign policy continues to reflect the nation's long-standing principles of power projection and exceptionalism.

  • Rebrand or Rebland? How Brand Image Reflects Appreciation for Consumers
    Business Psychology

    ABSTRACT: In recent years, many companies' brandings and outward appearances have become rather alike, following trends of minimalism, such as flat shapes and basic colors. This has mostly been pioneered by tech companies such as Google and Microsoft but is now also being seen in other businesses like PayPal or Jaguar. These new rebrands wildly contrast with many of the detailed and unique aesthetics seen in previous decades, which has spawned backlash. Many detractors view these rebrands as boring, unimaginative, and inferior compared to what came before. This paper aims to explore what factors have pushed this recent trend as well as the overall effect these rebrands have on consumers and employees. Do they truly help to revitalize a company's brand, or are they a sign of a growing disconnect between companies and their customers?

    By Stephen Lovas
  • Family Perspectives of Alzheimer's Dementia Progression & Types of Care: A Qualitative Methods Approach
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: In just under forty years from now, the United States will be faced with a crisis of dementia as our generations continue to age. Alzheimer's Dementia and its related diseases require around the clock care as the disease progresses, and abuse is a well documented issue in facilities that provide long term care for these patients. Documentation is much more scarce for people receiving treatment at home. Using a qualitative method study design with narrative approaches, one-on-one interviews will be conducted on family members of patients with Alzheimer's Dementia to analyze possible differences in quality and scope of care, with the hope that this research can highlight inequities in long term care.

    By Robin Stone
  • The Impact of Social Media on Teen Mental Health: A Mixed-Method Intervention
    Psychology Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: This mixed-method study investigates how social media interactions—particularly social comparison and online validation—affect adolescent mental health. With smartphones and social media now deeply embedded in teen life, concerns have grown about their psychological impact, including links to depression, anxiety, and low self-esteem. This study will recruit 150–200 high school students (ages 13–17) in Renton, Washington, to explore these dynamics through surveys, journal entries, and interviews. The findings may reveal that certain types of social media interactions negatively impact teen mental health, informing interventions such as digital mindfulness tools or policy changes to promote healthier online habits.

    By Kelsey Sugita
  • Breast Cancer: A Qualitative Study on Why Barriers Prevent North African Women with Stage III Breast Cancer from Seeking Mammography Screening and How to Overcome It Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: Breast cancer is the leading cause of cancer-related deaths among women worldwide, with particularly high mortality rates in North Africa due to late-stage diagnoses. This study aims to explore the barriers that prevent North African women from seeking mammography screening and to identify culturally appropriate strategies to overcome these challenges. Using a qualitative phenomenological approach, the study will involve in-depth interviews with ten North African women diagnosed with Stage III breast cancer. Anticipated themes include limited awareness, mistrust in the healthcare system, financial constraints, cultural beliefs, and fear of stigma. The findings aim to support the development of targeted public health campaigns and improve healthcare provider education in North African communities.

    By Mahy Hussein
  • A Comprehensive Analysis: The Relationship Between Temperature and Precipitation on Chokecherries Biology

    ABSTRACT: This research aimed to study the relationship and combined impact of temperature and precipitation on the overall fruit yield of Chokecherry trees, with the goal of finding conditions that maximize production and quality amidst constantly changing climate conditions. The hypothesis was that in order to maximize yield, trees needed spring temperatures ranging from 70°F to 85°F during growing seasons, and 32°F to 45°F during dormancy. Using data from the USA National Phenology Network (2014–2023), results suggest minimal correlation between precipitation and yield, maximum correlation between temperature and yield, and no collective correlation between temperature and precipitation on yield.

    By Gurkamal Kaur
  • Rodentia Species Did Not Follow the Latitudinal Diversity Gradient During the Miocene Biology

    ABSTRACT: The latitudinal diversity gradient (LDG) suggests that species richness increases as they get closer to the equator, but it is unclear whether this trend applies to rodents. Using data from the Paleobiology Database, a linear regression analysis yielded a p-value of 0.619, indicating no statistically significant relationship between latitudinal range and species richness. The results suggest that the LDG does not apply to rodents during the Miocene, and that plate tectonic activity — which led rodents to adapt to various environments — explains their distribution better than temperature changes alone.

    By Yordanos Berhane
  • Effects of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum on Freshwater Plant Distribution and its Relation to Modern Climate Change Biology

    ABSTRACT: This research tests the hypothesis that the distribution of plants in freshwater aquatic environments changed because of a rapidly warming climate during the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum. Comparing aquatic and terrestrial plant species between the Paleocene and Eocene epochs via a χ² test revealed a notable increase in aquatic plant distribution from the Paleocene to the Eocene — potentially resulting in impaired water quality, eutrophication, and a decline in aquatic biodiversity. Given our dependence on freshwater resources, similar trends in response to present-day climate change could pose significant risks to life on Earth.

    By Victoria Jeffords
  • Benthic Foraminifera Found to Have Survived Better During the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum with Implications to a Future Mass Extinction Event Biology

    ABSTRACT: This research tests whether planktonic foraminifera were greater victims of the global extinction event during the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) than benthic foraminifera, due to their exposure to warming in the open water column. A χ² test applied to data from the Paleobiology Database showed that benthic foraminifera frequency increased from the Paleocene to the Eocene while planktonic foraminifera decreased — suggesting benthic forams survived better during the PETM because they are less exposed to surface-level warming events, with implications for understanding future mass extinction risks.

    By Liz Macias
  • Students Eventually Value Active Learning
    Education Psychology

    ABSTRACT: This research investigates STEM students' feelings towards active learning methodologies in foundational physics and mathematics courses through an analysis of Student Evaluations of Teaching (SET) open-ended questions. Findings demonstrate a consistent increase in student appreciation for active learning environments over three quarters. The research highlights that educators who engage with more advanced students tend to achieve higher satisfaction compared to those teaching newer students, suggesting that prolonged exposure to active learning positively correlates with increased student satisfaction.

    By Parbeen Sekhon & Eric Onnen
