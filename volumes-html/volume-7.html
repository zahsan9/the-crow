Letter From the Editors

The Seventh Edition of The CROW was composed and published during the ongoing COVID-19 global pandemic, where universities pivoted between online and in-person learning. During this time, members of the University of Washington Bothell community continued to feel the pandemic's impact in every aspect of their lives, from the health and well-being of their families, friends, and communities to financial and job insecurity. Simultaneously, this time has been marked by incidents of global violence that have profound impact on the well-being of individuals, cultures, and nations globally. We have felt these impacts here at UW Bothell as well. We would like to acknowledge the hardship and the loss this past year has brought to people both near and far.

With this edition, The CROW continues to highlight the incredible dedication of the students at UW Bothell as they conduct, analyze, and synthesize their own research investigations in topics ranging from science and technology, to interdisciplinary arts, health studies, and everything in between. The act of conducting research is a high-impact learning practice that engages students outside of the classroom setting and allows them to think more critically about the topics they wish to discover and the world around them. By taking the initiative to submit their work for review by a board of their peers, the authors in this journal have taken steps towards becoming active contributors to academic discourse that values inclusion, expansion, and critical thinking.

Due to the events, new and ongoing, that defined the 2021-22 academic year, we would like to extend special recognition and gratitude to our student writers. In addition to the personal risk each writer might experience in submitting their work for peer review, the students who worked with us this year did so through a unique set of global circumstances. We know submitting during this time must have been difficult, and we appreciate your patience and your effort. We are so excited we can share with you the 2022 Edition of The CROW!

2022 Publications

  • Changing The Landscape of America's Dairyland: the Dominance of Holstein-Friesian Milk Products and Why this Breed no Longer Supports the Needs of the People Biology

    ABSTRACT: The classic dairy cow has a black and white hide: the classic appearance of the Holstein-Friesian breed. However, this breed of cattle may no longer be the most appropriate dairy cattle breed. This research essay compares three breeds of milk cow (Holstein-Friesian, Brown Swiss, Jersey), each with distinctly different physical characteristics and milk composition. Holstein-Friesian milk has the least fat and protein content of the three breeds, negatively impacting cheese yield. It would be beneficial to dairy farmers to switch breeds to either Brown Swiss or Jersey because of their smaller body size and higher composition of fats and proteins in their milk compared to Holstein-Friesian.

    By April Oertle
  • Literature as an Activist Tool to Spark Conversations
    Literature Political Science

    ABSTRACT: When asked to think about activism and social change, what comes to mind are activists, political speeches, protests, and the notion of creating movements that bring people together to lend voice to their ideas. What comes before these acts of activism and social change are that they originate from ideas. There is the belief that activist art does not have power and that people should become politicians and not artists if they want change. Art by itself may not do much work on its own, but where activist art contains power is that it can act as an initial spark for ideas — the realization that there is an aspect within society that needs to be brought to attention and changed. Literature is one example of art that has this kind of potential.

    By Raelynne Woo
  • Existential Dread on Pro-Environmental Behavior in Young Adults
    Psychology Political Science

    ABSTRACT: The effects of climate change encompass more than its ramifications on the natural environment. It's become important to analyze the effects of climate change discourse and action on the mental health of young adults. This paper analyzes the current mental health landscape of young adults, the classification of climate change as ambiguous loss associated with existential dread, and the effects of hope and coping mechanisms on decision making. The proposed study aims to determine a threshold of existential dread, classified as ecoanxiety, that would elicit pro-environmental behavior from young adults — contributing towards a novel theoretical framework for understanding the psychological load that climate change has on the developing psyche of future generations.

    By Jade Kanui Roque
  • Impacts of Birth Modality on Rates of Postpartum Depression: An Explanatory Sequential Mixed Method Study
    Health Studies Psychology

    ABSTRACT: Postpartum Depression (PPD) is the most common mental illness after childbirth, with long lasting and often severe effects on both birthing parents and offspring. Some modes of birth are associated with higher rates of PPD but the link is controversial and not well understood. Using an explanatory sequential mixed method design, phase one is a case control study examining quantitative data comparing PPD in people who experience anticipated and unanticipated birth modalities. Phase two consists of phenomenological interviews to uncover the emotional relationship between birth modality and PPD. Deepening the understanding of PPD causality is imperative to direct future research and develop effective and innovative treatments.

    By Fallon Zollars
  • Cognitive Development in Young Children during the Era of Mask Wearing: the Role of Emotion Recognition in Pediatric Brain Developmental Abilities
    Psychology Education

    ABSTRACT: Facial expressions are arguably one of the most critical social stimuli that allow infants and young children to communicate emotion and cognitively develop in areas of speech, language, and learning comprehension. With masks covering a great portion of an individual's face in daycares, schools, and everyday settings, the lack of facial cues becomes a great concern for how infants and young children develop during the era of mask wearing. This proposal explores the correlation between mask-wearing frequency and emotion recognition skills in children using a cross-sectional study design with an online survey and emotion recognition task, to yield a more prominent understanding of pediatric cognitive development associated with the social impacts of COVID-19.

    By Vi Nguyen
  • Negative Impacts Stemming from the Lack of Sexual Education in Autistic People Assigned Female at Birth: An Explanatory Mixed Method Study
    Health Studies Psychology Education

    ABSTRACT: Sexual education has been deficient in serving the needs of mainstream and marginalized populations, rife with misinformation about sexual health. This negatively impacts especially marginalized groups such as autistic women and nonbinary people who are more likely to be sexually assaulted than the general population. This study uses a mixed methods explanatory design to gather quantitative data through surveys and qualitative data through interviewing autistic women and nonbinary people about their sexual education experiences and what they want for the future of disability-informed sexual education. This study prides itself on its commitment to autistic participant involvement throughout the entire process, which is novel and will improve the quality of intervention and sexual health outcomes in the future.

    By Ellie Henry
  • Improving Algorithm and Modular Programming in the Search of EHVO in SDSS Quasar Spectra
    Physics Astronomy CSSE

    ABSTRACT: Quasars, the most luminous and energetic of Active Galactic Nuclei, are powered by a supermassive black hole with an accretion disk that creates wind-like outflows. An outflow with speeds between 10–20% of the speed of light is classified as an Extremely High Velocity Outflow (EHVO). We searched for EHVOs by developing automated algorithms to normalize spectra and search for broad Carbon IV absorption lines in quasar spectra from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey Data Release 16. The number of spectra available is rapidly growing with each data release, making it vital to have accurate algorithms to limit manual work. Here we discuss improvements made to our algorithms and how we designed the code to be easily accessible to other researchers and future undergraduate students continuing this project.

    By Mikel Charles, Dakota Bunger, Cora DeFrancesco, Wendy García Naranjo, Nathnael Kahassai, Michael Parker, & Anish Saurav Rijal
  • Self-reported Precautionary COVID-19 Measures Taken by Hikers, Mountain Bikers, and Trail Runners in California During Spring 2020
    Health Studies Political Science

    ABSTRACT: To slow COVID-19's progress in California, Governor Newsom issued an executive order on March 19, 2020, implementing a stay-at-home order. This study sought to describe and compare social distancing habits and the use of personal protective equipment (PPE) by people participating in outdoor sports in California's public open spaces during the pandemic. Of all respondents (n=822), 87% reported distancing of greater than 1.8 m around others on trails. However, 56% of mountain bikers, 48% of trail runners, and 35% of hikers reported not wearing a facial covering during outdoor exercise, and 71% did not worry about COVID-19 exposure while outdoors.

    By April Oertle
  • Is There Magic in the Table or the Screen: A Look at the Effects of Digitalization on Table-top-role-playing-games
    Literature Psychology

    ABSTRACT: This paper examines the transition of table-top role-playing games to online platforms like Discord due to the circumstances of the COVID pandemic, using Dungeons and Dragons and Call of Cthulhu as prime examples. After introducing the concept of digital disengagement, the paper delves into the history of both games and the evolution of computer technology that made the transition online possible, then examines the differences between in-person and online play by synthesizing personal experiences with the research of other scholars within and related to this topic.

    By Sam Buck
  • Survive-at-Home Orders: COVID-19 Policy Through the Lens of Intimate Partner Violence ⚠ Content Warning
    Political Science Health Studies

    ABSTRACT: Two years into the pandemic, the consequences of early United States COVID-19 policies on victims of intimate partner violence (IPV) remain largely hidden from view. This literature review of over 40 sources examines how policies like stay-at-home orders and the stalled renewal of the Violence Against Women Act, compounded by a lack of economic relief, may have influenced the risks associated with IPV. The findings highlight a dire, life-threatening crisis that was perpetuated and prolonged by the government's initial pandemic responses and reduced funding for victim services. In addition to renewing VAWA, the paper recommends investing heavily in prevention programs, as adopting this recommendation before the next disaster strikes may mitigate rippling intergenerational impacts.

    By Tori Satterfield
  • Evidence that During the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum Plesiadapiform Primates did not Follow the Latitudinal Diversity Gradient Biology

    ABSTRACT: A warming event occurred fifty-six million years ago on the border of the Paleocene and Eocene, known as the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum. Many animals suffered mass extinction, but primates increased in abundance and diversity. This study examines whether there is a significant difference in the number of species of Plesiadapiform primates at higher latitudinal zones in the Eocene than in the Paleocene. A χ² test found that the number of species at higher latitudinal zones is significantly greater in the Eocene than in the Paleocene — the LDG was lacking during the PETM, which allowed primates to migrate to higher latitudes because Earth's polar caps completely melted and the tropical climate expanded far beyond the equatorial zone.

    By Isabel-Yelena Andreev
  • Do Ghost Net Entanglement Rates Vary Among Sea Turtle Species? Comparative Analysis of Regional Studies Provides Insight
    Biology Political Science

    ABSTRACT: Ghost nets — fishing nets that have been lost, abandoned, or carelessly discarded — entangle sea turtles, reducing their population sizes and leading to faster spread of disease. Comparing and evaluating five original research papers, data collected over 14 years shows that 9,989 ghost nets entangled 1,374 sea turtles across all species and locations. Olive ridleys had the highest rate of entanglement at 132 individuals per year in the Maldives. What these findings suggest is that the impact of ghost nets on global sea turtle populations is larger than previously expected, with species entangled more frequently being at increased risk of becoming endangered — highlighting the urgent need for updated fishing net regulations.

    By Helen Fita
  • Deconstructing Realism: Narrative Authority, Straddling Liminality, and the (Non)Fantastical in Restorative Poetics Literature

    ABSTRACT: As authors, poets, and artists, we are both inclined and encouraged to dive into the wellspring of our personal lives, inevitably incorporating real life experiences and people into our works. This essay explores objectivity, subjectivity, and authorship — who owns a story — using thought pieces from artist and editor Katherine Behar's Object-Oriented Feminism, as well as the interconnectivity of these concepts in magical realism, absurdity, and fantasy in order to consider an author's moral obligation to others during their own restorative and artistic journey. Are these experiences ethically responsible to publish when we might heal from letting them live in a private journal, removing all potential harm to others the stories may cause?

    By Alysa Levi-D'Ancona
  • Comparing the Efficacy of Pregabalin and Gabapentin in Treating Neuropathic Pain
    Health Studies Biology

    ABSTRACT: Neuropathic pain affects an estimated 10% of the United States population. While opioids have been observed to provide pain relief, they pose high risks of dependency with potentially fatal results. This paper compares the efficacy of pregabalin and gabapentin in treating neuropathic pain using four existing empirical research publications. Gabapentin was found to reduce pain by 1.5–2.5 units on the Likert scale, while pregabalin reduced pain by 1.4–1.67 units. Pregabalin appears to have similar analgesic effects at lower doses compared to gabapentin, which may indicate greater efficacy — though this inference needs more rigorous research to be fully established.

    By Jacqueline Monteza
